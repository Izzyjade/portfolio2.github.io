Muon Telescope

In the Summer of 2012, I participated in an intership program through the Particle Physics Department at UCSC with 10 other local students. We spent the first week learning about particle physics from professors in the department, and then were given particle detectors and asked to do some sort of experiment measuring muons. My partner and I decided to investigate which angle muons hit the earth at most frequently. In order to do this, we made a telescope out of three detectors using scrap materials in the lab. We did our measurements from the roof of a parking garage to minimize interference. We started with the telescope pointed straight up and timed it for an hour. Then we changed the angle by 15 degrees and went all the way to 0 degrees in each cardinal direction. We ended up finding that most muons come from around 90 degrees from the horizon, most likely because they are less likely to decay from that direction since there is less atmosphere to travel through.
This project was my first experience with engineering and helped me discover my passion for it!
